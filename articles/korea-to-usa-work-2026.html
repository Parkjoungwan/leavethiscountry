Working in the United States is a common aspiration for Korean professionals, but it's one of the most complex immigration systems in the world. South Korea has a strong bilateral relationship with the US, and Korean nationals have access to several visa pathways — from the lottery-based H-1B to the investment-based E-2 Treaty Investor Visa that is exclusively available to Korean nationals due to a bilateral treaty.

H-1B Lottery: The Main Route for Professionals

The H-1B is the primary work visa for skilled professionals in the US. It requires employer sponsorship and is subject to an annual cap of 85,000 (65,000 regular + 20,000 for US master's holders). The lottery selection rate has dropped to roughly 20% in recent years.

  • Employer must file a petition (Form I-129) with USCIS on your behalf
  • Registration period: typically March each year, for October 1st start
  • Initial approval: 3 years, renewable for 3 more years
  • Fee: $460–$730+ (employer pays; premium processing adds ~$2,805)

E-2 Treaty Investor Visa: Korea's Advantage

South Korea has a bilateral investment treaty with the US, making Korean nationals eligible for the E-2 Treaty Investor Visa — unavailable to many other nationalities. This allows Koreans who invest substantially in a US business to live and work in the US.

  • No fixed minimum investment — must be "substantial" relative to the business (typically $100,000–$500,000)
  • Must own at least 50% of the business or have operational control
  • Valid for 5 years, renewable indefinitely
  • Does not lead directly to Green Card (permanent residency)

Key Tips for Korean Applicants

  • If applying for H-1B, get a job offer at a large US company early — they have dedicated immigration teams and are familiar sponsors
  • Korean tech professionals working at US-based Korean companies (Samsung America, Hyundai Motor America, LG Electronics) may be eligible for L-1 visas
  • Visa-free entry for tourism under VWP (Visa Waiver Program) — Koreans can visit the US for 90 days without a visa, but cannot work on this status
  • Green Card (permanent residency) for employment-based immigrants typically takes 3–10+ years from Korea
