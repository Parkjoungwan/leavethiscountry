The TN Visa — Americans' Superpower

Under CUSMA (formerly NAFTA), Americans can work in Canada in 60+ specified professions without a Labor Market Impact Assessment (LMIA). No months-long employer sponsorship process — you present at a Canadian border crossing or airport with a job offer letter and your credentials, and CBSA issues a 1-year TN work permit on the spot. It's renewable indefinitely with no cap on renewals.

What to Bring to the Border

  • Valid US passport
  • Job offer letter from Canadian employer (on company letterhead, stating role, salary, duration)
  • Credentials proof: degree certificates, professional licenses, or reference letters confirming experience
  • CAD $155 application fee (credit card accepted at most crossings)

Non-TN Professions: LMIA Work Permit

If your job isn't TN-eligible, your employer must obtain an LMIA proving no qualified Canadian is available. Processing: 2–6 months, employer bears the cost (~CAD $1,000 fee). Tech companies with global talent stream (GTS) LMIA get 2-week expedited processing. Once LMIA is approved, you apply for a work permit from inside or outside Canada.

US Tax Warning: A TN work permit does not end your US tax filing obligation. You still owe the IRS an annual return on worldwide income. See the Immigration section for full details on FBAR, FATCA, and the Canada-US Tax Treaty.

Why Americans Choose Canada for Education

Canadian university tuition for international students (CAD $25K–40K/year) is significantly cheaper than comparable US private universities ($55K–80K USD/year). McGill, University of Toronto, and UBC rank in global top 35. Programs are taught in English. Credits and degrees are fully recognized by US employers and graduate schools.

Study Permit Process

  1. Receive acceptance letter from a Designated Learning Institution (DLI)
  2. Apply online at IRCC — upload passport, proof of funds, acceptance letter
  3. Americans do not need to provide a biometric if applying from inside Canada; from outside, one biometric appointment required (CAD $85)
  4. Processing: 4–8 weeks online, faster with a Student Direct Stream if eligible
  5. Upon approval, study permit is issued at the Canadian port of entry on arrival

Work Rights During Study

Study permit holders can work up to 24 hours/week off-campus during semesters and full-time during scheduled breaks. On-campus work has no hour limit. Spouses of international students in master's or PhD programs qualify for an open work permit.

Post-Study: PGWP to PR Pipeline

After graduating from a DLI program of 8+ months, you qualify for a Post-Graduation Work Permit (PGWP) — open work permit (any employer) for up to 3 years matching your program length. This Canadian work experience then qualifies you for Canadian Experience Class (CEC) in Express Entry, which has among the lowest CRS cutoffs (~450–470 in recent draws). This is the most reliable path from student → permanent resident → citizen for Americans.

Express Entry: CRS Score Reality for Americans

Americans without Canadian work experience typically land at CRS 420–470. Recent general draws cut off around 480–530, which means most Americans need either:

  • Canadian Experience Class (CEC): 1 year of skilled work in Canada (NOC TEER 0, 1, 2, or 3) → CRS jumps to 450–500+, CEC draws often invite at 430–460
  • Provincial Nominee Program (PNP): Province nominates you → +600 CRS points → guaranteed ITA at next draw
  • Spousal bonus: Spouse with strong credentials adds 40 CRS points

Provincial Nominee Programs (PNP) for Americans

PNP streams that work well for Americans:

  • Ontario Immigrant Nominee Program (OINP) — Tech Draw: Targets IT workers; invitations go to Express Entry profiles with tech NOC codes
  • BC PNP Tech: Fast-tracked for 29 in-demand tech occupations; employer must be registered in BC
  • Alberta Advantage Immigration Program (AAIP): Active labour market with engineering/energy demand; no job offer required for some streams
  • Atlantic Immigration Program: All 4 Atlantic provinces (Nova Scotia, NB, PEI, Newfoundland); lower cost of living, easier pathway, job offer required

Spousal Sponsorship

If you're in a relationship with a Canadian citizen or PR, spousal sponsorship is a direct, non-competitive path. Processing: 12–18 months for inland sponsorship (both already in Canada) or 12 months for outland (you apply from the US). No CRS score involved.

Critical: US Tax Obligations Don't Stop at the Border

The US is one of only two countries (with Eritrea) that taxes citizens on worldwide income. Moving to Canada does not end your IRS filing obligation. Key obligations:

  • Annual US tax return (Form 1040): Required every year regardless of where you live or how long you've been in Canada
  • FBAR (FinCEN 114): Required if any Canadian bank accounts (chequing, savings, TFSA, RRSP) exceed $10,000 USD equivalent at any point during the year. Failure penalty: up to $10,000 per violation (non-willful)
  • FATCA (Form 8938): Additional IRS reporting for foreign financial assets above $200K (married filing jointly, living abroad)
  • Canada-US Tax Treaty: Prevents true double taxation — you claim a Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116) for Canadian taxes paid. Since Canada's marginal rates (~53% in Ontario at top bracket) exceed US rates (~37% federal), most Americans owe little or no additional US tax on Canadian employment income
  • RRSP recognition: The treaty recognizes Canadian RRSPs as tax-deferred retirement accounts for US purposes — you can hold RRSPs without annual US tax on growth (Form 8891 was eliminated; attach treaty disclosure to Form 1040)

Hire a cross-border tax specialist (CPA licensed in both jurisdictions) immediately upon establishing Canadian residency. Cost: USD $1,500–4,000/year but prevents $10K–100K+ penalties.

Healthcare in Canada — What Americans Need to Know

Provincial health insurance is the biggest lifestyle upgrade Americans describe after moving. No premiums, no deductibles, no copays for covered services (GP visits, specialist referrals, hospital stays, surgery). But there are critical transition details:

  • 3-month waiting period: Most provinces (Ontario, BC, Manitoba) don't cover you until you've been a resident for 3 months. Alberta and some others start on arrival day.
  • Private bridging insurance: Buy temporary health insurance for the waiting period. Budget CAD $150–350/month for an individual, $400–800 for a family.
  • What's NOT covered: Dental, vision, and most prescription drugs are excluded from provincial plans. Most working Canadians get supplemental benefits through employers. Individual plans: ~CAD $100–200/month.
  • US Medicare/Medicaid: Does not cover you in Canada. Enroll in provincial coverage and drop US private insurance once covered.

Driver's License

Most provinces recognize US driving experience and give full credit toward the graduated licensing system. In Ontario, you can exchange a US license for a full G license within 90 days of establishing residency (no road test required if your US license is from a reciprocal state). BC, Alberta, and Quebec have similar exchange agreements. Keep your US license during the transition.

Dual Citizenship

Canada allows dual citizenship — you can become a Canadian citizen without renouncing your US passport. The US technically allows this too (though the oath of citizenship in some countries triggers a US expatriation process; Canada's oath does not). You can hold both passports indefinitely. Renouncing US citizenship is a separate, irreversible decision (Exit Tax on assets over $2M) — not required and rarely advisable.

eTA is valid for 5 years or until your passport expires, whichever comes first. One eTA covers unlimited trips. Apply at canada.ca/en/immigration-refugees-citizenship/services/visit-canada/eta/apply.html — do not use third-party sites that charge extra fees.

NEXUS Card — Worth It for Frequent Crossers

NEXUS is a joint US-Canada trusted traveler program. $50 USD, 5-year validity. Benefits: dedicated NEXUS lanes at land crossings (no waiting), Global Entry equivalent in the US, and serves as eTA for flights into Canada. Application requires background check + in-person interview at a NEXUS enrollment center. Processing time varies: 2–6 months.

Visiting vs. Staying

CBSA officers determine how long you can stay (default is 6 months). If you're planning to stay long-term as a visitor, be aware that Canada and the US share entry/exit records — if you're present for more than 183 days in Canada in a calendar year, you may be considered a Canadian tax resident, triggering Canadian tax filing obligations. Consult a cross-border tax advisor before extended stays.

Practical Travel Info

  • Currency: CAD is typically 25–30% weaker than USD — good purchasing power for Americans visiting
  • Healthcare while visiting: Your US health insurance may cover emergency care in Canada as out-of-network. Verify before you go; travel insurance is recommended regardless.
  • Cell service: Most US carrier plans (T-Mobile, Verizon, AT&T) include Canada in their plan. Check your plan's Canada data roaming terms.
  • Alcohol + cannabis: Cannabis is federally legal in Canada — you cannot bring any across the US border (federal crime in both directions, even from states where it's legal)
