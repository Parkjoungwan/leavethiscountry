Japan is the most visited international destination for South Koreans — and for obvious reasons. A 2-hour flight, zero visa hassle, 90-day visa-free entry, and a deeply complementary culture make Japan the ultimate weekend (or month-long) escape for Korean passport holders.

Entry Requirements: What You Need

Korean citizens can enter Japan with zero paperwork. No visa application, no pre-registration, no fees:

  • Valid Korean passport — must be valid for the duration of your stay
  • Return or onward ticket — technically required, though rarely checked
  • Proof of sufficient funds — rarely checked at immigration
  • Accommodation address — have hotel details on hand for immigration form

At the border, you'll fill out a disembarkation card and go through standard immigration. For Korean citizens, this typically takes under 10 minutes.

The 90-Day Rule

You can stay in Japan for up to 90 days per visit. There is technically no minimum time you must spend outside Japan before re-entering, but immigration officers have discretion. If you appear to be living in Japan on tourist status, you may be refused entry.

  • Multiple entries allowed — you can visit as many times per year as you like
  • Each visit is assessed independently at immigration
  • Extended stays: if you need longer, look into a Working Holiday visa or cultural activity visa

Korean Travelers in Japan: Practical Notes

  • Language: Japanese is the primary language, but Korean signage is common in tourist areas (Osaka, Kyoto, Tokyo's Korean-heavy neighborhoods)
  • Payment: Japan is still cash-heavy — have yen on hand. IC cards (Suica, Pasmo) work on all transit and at many stores
  • SIM/WiFi: Pocket WiFi rental at the airport or prepaid data SIM (IIJmio, Mobal) — no Korean SIM works on Japanese frequencies without roaming
  • Safety: Japan is consistently one of the safest countries globally — petty crime is extremely rare

Best Times to Visit

  • Cherry blossom (March–April): Most popular — book accommodation 3–6 months in advance
  • Autumn foliage (October–November): Stunning colors, cooler weather, less crowded than spring
  • Winter (December–February): Ski season in Hokkaido, fewer tourists in Kyoto/Nara, great food festivals
  • Avoid: Golden Week (late April–early May) and Obon (mid-August) — extremely crowded and expensive
