International Experience Canada (IEC) Working Holiday

The IEC Working Holiday stream is by far the most popular route for Japanese workers under 35. Canada allocates approximately 6,500 spots per year to Japanese nationals. The application fee is CAD 150, and successful applicants receive an open work permit valid for up to 12 months, allowing them to work for any employer anywhere in Canada. Applications are submitted online through IRCC (Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada), and invitations are issued via pool draws throughout the year.

Japanese applicants should prepare a valid passport, proof of funds (at least CAD 2,500), and travel or health insurance. One important consideration: Japan's weak yen in recent years has made Canadian salaries highly attractive, and many young Japanese professionals use the WHV as a gateway to explore permanent residency pathways while building Canadian work experience.

Employer-Sponsored Work Permits

Beyond the IEC, skilled workers can obtain Canadian work permits through employer sponsorship. The most common route is the Labour Market Impact Assessment (LMIA) process, where a Canadian employer must demonstrate that no qualified Canadian worker is available. Processing times vary by province and occupation. Certain occupations listed under the Global Talent Stream are exempt from LMIA and can be processed in as little as two weeks.

  • Global Talent Stream (Category A & B): For highly-skilled tech workers; employer-specific permit, no LMIA required
  • LMIA-based work permit: Any occupation; employer bears the CAD 1,000 LMIA fee
  • Intra-Company Transfer (ICT): Japanese companies with Canadian offices can transfer employees under CUSMA rules

Express Entry (Work While Applying)

Japanese nationals with a valid job offer and sufficient points can enter the Express Entry pool and receive an Invitation to Apply (ITA) for permanent residency. Canadian Experience Class (CEC) candidates who have accumulated one year of skilled work experience in Canada under the IEC or LMIA permit are particularly competitive. English proficiency (CLB 7+ typically required) is the main barrier for many Japanese applicants, making language preparation essential before departure.

Student Direct Stream (SDS)

Japan is eligible for Canada's Student Direct Stream (SDS), which offers faster processing (typically within 20 days) compared to the regular study permit stream. To qualify, Japanese students must obtain a Guaranteed Investment Certificate (GIC) of at least CAD 10,000 from a participating Canadian financial institution, achieve an IELTS score of 6.0 or higher on each band, and have a letter of acceptance from a Designated Learning Institution (DLI).

Tuition at Canadian universities ranges from CAD 20,000 to CAD 45,000 per year for international students depending on the program and institution. Living costs in Toronto or Vancouver add another CAD 15,000–20,000 annually. Scholarships through the Vanier Canada Graduate Scholarships and institutional awards are available but competitive.

Post-Graduation Work Permit (PGWP)

One of Canada's strongest incentives for international students is the Post-Graduation Work Permit (PGWP). After graduating from an eligible DLI, students can apply for an open work permit valid for the length of their study program, up to a maximum of 3 years. This Canadian work experience then becomes a direct pathway into Express Entry's Canadian Experience Class, making Canada one of the most straightforward study-to-immigration pipelines for Japanese nationals.

Express Entry

Canada's Express Entry system is the primary federal pathway to permanent residency. It manages three programs: Federal Skilled Worker (FSW), Canadian Experience Class (CEC), and Federal Skilled Trades (FST). Points are awarded under the Comprehensive Ranking System (CRS) based on age, education, language proficiency, and work experience. For Japanese applicants, the biggest challenge is typically achieving strong IELTS or CELPIP scores, as English education in Japan historically focuses on reading over speaking.

Japanese applicants who have completed Canadian education or have Canadian work experience through IEC tend to score significantly higher in the CRS pool. Category-based draws for specific occupations — including healthcare, STEM, and trades — have been introduced in recent years, which can benefit Japanese professionals in those fields even with lower overall CRS scores.

Provincial Nominee Programs (PNP)

All Canadian provinces and territories (except Quebec, which runs its own system) operate Provincial Nominee Programs that can accelerate the immigration process. Nominees receive an additional 600 CRS points, effectively guaranteeing an ITA. Provinces like British Columbia, Alberta, and Ontario have streams specifically targeting skilled workers and international graduates. Japanese nationals interested in French-speaking Quebec can explore the Quebec Skilled Worker Program (QSWP) with French language skills.

Atlantic Immigration Program & Rural Pilots

For those open to living outside major cities, the Atlantic Immigration Program (AIP) offers a pathway through New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, PEI, and Newfoundland. These regions are actively recruiting skilled workers and international graduates. Rural and Northern Immigration Pilots in smaller communities also offer targeted pathways with employer endorsement requirements. These programs can be significantly less competitive than the main Express Entry pool.

Electronic Travel Authorization (eTA)

Japanese passport holders do not require a visitor visa to enter Canada. However, all visa-exempt travelers arriving by air must obtain an Electronic Travel Authorization (eTA) before boarding their flight. The eTA costs CAD 7, is valid for 5 years or until the passport expires (whichever comes first), and can be used for multiple entries. Applications are completed online through the Government of Canada website and are usually approved within minutes, though applicants are advised to apply at least 72 hours before departure.

The eTA is linked electronically to your passport and does not require a physical stamp or sticker. Border officers will typically grant a stay of up to 6 months upon arrival, though the actual duration is at the discretion of the CBSA officer. Travelers arriving by land from the United States do not need an eTA.

What to Expect at Canadian Customs

  • Canadian customs uses the CBSA eDeclaration app for paperless declarations — download before your flight
  • Currency over CAD 10,000 (or equivalent) must be declared
  • Certain foods, plants, and animal products may be restricted or require declaration
  • Biometrics (fingerprints and photo) are required on first entry if not previously given

Popular Destinations and Travel Tips

Canada is enormous — domestic flights between cities like Tokyo→Vancouver→Toronto can span thousands of kilometers. Japan Airlines and Air Canada operate direct flights between Tokyo Narita and Vancouver (approximately 9–10 hours) and Toronto (13 hours). The most popular tourist regions for Japanese visitors include Vancouver's natural scenery, Banff National Park in Alberta, and Niagara Falls in Ontario. Winter travel to Québec City is particularly popular among Japanese tourists seeking authentic French Canadian culture and winter festivals. Travel health insurance is strongly recommended as Canadian healthcare is not free for visitors.
